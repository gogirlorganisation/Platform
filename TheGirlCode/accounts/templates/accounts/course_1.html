Programming · X 2017

Welcome to Day 1 of the Programming Event of MINET X 2017. We hope you have as much fun solving the problems as we had making them. You have been provided with Code::Blocks,Sublime Text and Turbo C++ environments.

The event will be two and a half hours long. There are 5 topics, each with three levels of difficulty - Easy, Medium and Hard. Your answers will have a direct bearing on your team’s stock prices. The score of every team will be relative to the team with the highest score.

Whenever you wish to attempt a question, raise your hand and one of us will come to you. Note that whenever there is an unsuccessful attempt, the maximum points for a problem are halved. So, for instance, if you answer a question perfectly on your third try, you get one-fourth of the maximum points. Accessing any other site other than the links provided and communication between two or more teams is not permitted.

If you have any questions about the problems, do not be afraid to ask us. Have fun, and Make beautiful programs. Best of luck!

Round One ·

Pascal’s Triangle

Given two integers $i$ and $j$, output all numbers between the $i^{th}$ and $j^{th}$ positions in the $n^{th}$ line of the Pascal’s Triangle. Note that the row number starts from 0.



Input:
The first line of input contains an integer $n$, the number of the line which is being tested. The next two lines contain the integers $i$ and $j$.

Output:
The program should output a list of integers between the $i^{th}$ and $j^{th}$ positions in the $n^{th}$ line of the Pascal’s Triangle.

Limit:
$3 \leq n \leq 30$ , $0 \leq i \leq n-1$ and $1 \leq j \leq n$

Example:

Input Output
5
0
3		 1 5 10 10

Under Cut

Pintu, the electrician has a big wire of length $N$ metres. He decides to cut this wire into small pieces of at most $K$ meters, for later use. Help Pintu by telling him what’s the minimum number of wires the big wire has to be cut into, and in how many ways can this be done.

Input:
Two space-separated integers $N$, $K$.

Output:
Two space separated integers, minimum number of wires and number of ways in which it can be cut modulo $10^{6}$.

Limits:
$1 \leq N,K \leq 10^{4}$

Example:

Input Output
7 3 3 6

Explanation:
The minimum number of wire is 3, and there are 6 ways to make 2 cuts:

  • Positions: 1 4
  • Positions: 3 4
  • Positions: 3 6
  • Positions: 2 4
  • Positions: 2 5
  • Positions: 3 5

Sweet Tooth

You have been given an array of coloured candies. All candies have the same features and can only be differentiated on the basis of their colour. Your task is to give us the total number of ways in which you can form a pack of $N$ candies from the given candies.

Input:
First line contains two space seperated integers $X$ and $N$ followed by $X$ integers in the next line denoting $A_1, A_2, ... , A_X$ - colours.

Output:
Output the required answer modulo $10^{5}$ in one line.

Limit:
$1 \leq N \leq X \leq 2 \times 10^{5}$ and $1 \leq A_i \leq 2 \times 10^5$

Example:

Input Output
4 2
3 2 2 3		 3
5 1
2 2 2 2 2		 1

Explanation:
 Input 1: Three possible ways are:

  • [2, 2]
  • [3, 3]
  • [2, 3]
Input 2: Only possible way is [2].

Dark Knights

Given a $n$-sided chessboard, determine the maximum number of stable knights i.e, the highest number of knights that can be placed such that no knight is able to kill another within a single move.

Input:
A single integer $n$.

Output:
A single integer that gives the maximum number of stable knights.

Limit:
$0 \leq n \leq 10^{5}$

Example:

Input Output

The Divisor Game

Sarkar and Bhavnani are playing a game which has the following rules:

  • Initially, there is a positive integer $n$ written on the desk, which is greater than 1
  • Sarkar makes the first move, then players make moves alternatively. On each move the current player can either decrease the number on the desk by 1, or divide it by one of its divisors except itself and 1. It's forbidden to decrease the number if it's equal to 1.
  • If the current player can't make a move, then he loses. In other words, if there is "1" written on the desk right before his move, then he loses. You need to determine the winner of the game, if $n$ is the number written on the desk initially. You may assume, that both players play optimally.

Input:
An integer $n$.

Output:
The name of the winner.

Limit:
$2 \leq n \leq 10^8$

Example:

Input Output
4 Sarkar

Right to win

Munish and Navjot are playing a game. The game is played on a horizontal strip consisting of $n$ cells that are numbered from left to right with consecutive integers from 1 to $n$.

At the beginning of the game, a pawn is placed at the cell numbered $x$. The players make moves alternately, i.e. Munish makes all odd numbered moves (1st, 3rd, 5th and so on) and Navjot all even numbered moves (2nd, 4th, 6th and so on). During the $k^{th}$ move the player should move the pawn either exactly $k$ cells to the left or exactly $k$ cells to the right (provided the pawn doesn't land outside the strip).

For example, in the $k^{th}$ move, if the pawn is located at cell $p$ before the move, it can be moved to either $(p - k)$ or $(p + k)$ if the appropriate cells are part of the strip.

The player who is not able to make a move (i.e. both the above choices lead to cells outside of the strip) loses the game. Assume that both the players play optimally. We want to find out who will win if the pawn is initially placed at $x$ in a strip containing $n$ cells if Munish plays first.

Input:
Input consists of two space seperated integers $n$ and $x$.

Output:
The winner i.e. Munish or Navjot.

Limits:
$1 \leq n \leq 10^{6}$

Example:

Input Output
3 2 Navjot
4 2 Munish

Mr. Bean’s Day Out

Our good friend, Mr. Bean, is very fond of playing games, and specifically enjoys playing whack-a-mole a lot. So he set out to make his own Whack - a - mole machine. He went to Mrs. Wicket, his landlady, who had $n$ moles in her garden. She wanted to get rid of them.

The moles have a certain strength which is defined by a natural number $s$.But he came across one problem: if the selected $k$ [>1] moles with strengths $(s_1,s_2,s_3...s_k)$ tend to fight among each other if gcd $(s_1,s_2,s_3...s_k)$ = 1, and hence wouldn’t allow Mr. Bean to play Whack- a - mole.

Given the fact that, more moles means more fun to play, help him to select the maximum number of moles he can choose from the $n$ moles so that they don’t fight amongst each other and allow our friend, Mr. Bean, to play Whack- a- mole happily.

Input:
The input consists of two lines.The first line contains an integer $n$ the number of moles available. The next line contains $n$ space separated integers, where the $i$th number denotes $s_i$ , the strength of the ith mole.

Output:
Print single integer — the maximum number of moles Mr. Bean can take, so that he can play Whack- a- mole.

Limits:
$1 \leq n, s_i \leq 10^5$

Example:

Input Output
3
2 3 4		 2

Social Network

Sidhant examines a social network (because he has no friends. Its main functionality is that two members can become friends (then they can talk with each other and share funny pictures).
There are $n$ members, numbered 1 through $n$ and $m$ pairs of members are friends. Of course, a member can't be a friend with themselves.
Let $A-B$ denote that members $A$ and $B$ are friends. Sidhant thinks that a network is reasonable if and only if the following condition is satisfied: For every three distinct members $(X, Y, Z)$,  if $X-Y$ and $Y-Z$ then also $X-Z$. For example: if Aryaman and Nishant are friends, and Aryaman and Rishi are friends, then Rishi and Nishant should be friends as well. Can you help Sidhant and check if the network is reasonable?

Input:
The first line of the input contain two integers $n$ and $m$ — the number of members and the number of pairs of members that are friends. The $i^{th}$ of the next $m$ lines contains two distinct integers $a_i$ and $b_i$ Members $a_i$ and $b_i$ are friends with each other. No pair of members will appear more than once in the input.

Output:
If the given network is reasonable, print "YES" in a single line (without the quotes). Otherwise, print "NO" in a single line (without the quotes). .

Limits:
$0 \leq n \leq 150000$
$0 \leq m \leq min(150000,\frac{n.(n-1)}{2})$
$1 \leq a_i, b_i \leq n$

Example:

Input Output
4 3
1 3
3 4
1 4 		YES
4 4
3 1
2 3
3 4
1 2 		NO
3 2
1 2
2 3 		NO

Quest for Laddus

Little boy Bheem wants to eat all kinds of laddus, and thus decides to travel. At first, he decided to visit all the cities of his motherland — Dholakpur.

It is widely known that Dholakpur has $n$ cities connected with $m$ bidirectional roads. Also, there are no two roads in the country that connect the same pair of cities, but roads starting and ending in the same city can exist. Bheem wants to plan his journey beforehand. He thinks a path is good if the path goes over $(m - 2)$ roads twice, and over the other 2 exactly once. The good path can start and finish in any city of Dholakpur. Now he wants to know how many different good paths are in Dholakpur.

Two paths are considered different if the sets of roads the paths goes over exactly once differ. Help Bheem — calculate the number of good paths.

Input:
The first line contains two integers $n$, $m$ — the number of cities and roads in Dholakpur.
Each of the next $m$ lines contains two integers $u$ and $v$. There is road between cities $u$ and $v$. It is guaranteed that no road will be given in the input twice. That also means that for every city there is no more than one road that connects the city to itself.

Output:
Print out the only integer — the number of good paths in Dholakpur.

Limits:
$0 < n,m < 10^6$ and $0< u,v< n$

Example:

Input Output
5 4 1 2
1 3
1 4
1 5 		6

Dijkstra wants to go home

The Königsberg bridge problem asks if the seven bridges of the city of Königsberg, formerly in Germany but now known as Kaliningrad and part of Russia, over the river Preger can all be traversed in a single trip without doubling back. Your job is to tell whether you can cover the said closed figure of $n$ in a single trip without doubling back.

Input:
First line of input is $n$ vertices, followed by an integer $k$. The next $k$ lines of inputs contain 2 space separated integers denoting the vertices that are connected. Note: There can be more than 1 edge joining 2 vertices.

Output:
"Yes" if it is possible to cover all edges else "No" if not possible.

Limits:
$0 < n \leq 100$ and $0 < k < 10^4$

Example:

Input Output
4 7
4 1
1 4
1 2
4 2
3 4
4 3
2 3 		No
4 5
1 3
2 3
1 4
2 4
3 4 		Yes
